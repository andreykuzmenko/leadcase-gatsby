## 🤯 Challenges with feedback

<p>Have you ever found yourself struggling to provide <strong>constructive feedback,</strong> either in a professional setting or in personal relationships?</p><p><strong>Feedback</strong> is a crucial tool for growth and improvement, but it's often challenging to deliver it in a way that's both effective and empathetic. This is where the <strong>COIN </strong>feedback framework comes into play. It offers a structured approach to giving feedback that is clear, specific, and actionable, enhancing the likelihood of positive outcomes and mutual understanding.</p>

## 💎 COIN model

<!-- image: /images/cards/bffca76dff5d59b.png -->

<p><span style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);">The </span><strong style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);">COIN framework</strong><span style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);"> is composed of four key components:</span></p><ol><li>Context</li><li>Observations</li><li>Impact</li><li>Next Steps</li></ol><p>Let's review each component in detail.</p>

## 👥 1. Context

<p><span style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);">The situation component is all about </span><strong style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);">setting the stage</strong><span style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);"> for the feedback. It involves describing the specific event or context in which the behavior was observed. This step is crucial as it situates the feedback in a real and relatable scenario, making it easier for the recipient to recall and understand the context.</span></p><p><strong style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);">Example</strong></p><p><em style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);">"Alex, I've noticed some issues with deadlines in recent projects that we need to address."</em></p>

## 👀 2. Observation

<p><span style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);">In this step, you detail the</span><strong style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);"> specific actions</strong><span style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);"> taken by the individual. The key here is to focus on observable behavior, avoiding any subjective judgments or assumptions about the person's intentions. By concentrating on what exactly happened, the feedback remains objective and straightforward.</span></p><p><strong style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);">Example</strong></p><p><em style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);">"The last few reports were submitted a couple of days late, like the marketing report due on the 10th but submitted on the 12th."</em></p>

## 😢 3. Impact

<p><span style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);">This part is about the </span><strong style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);">effects </strong><span style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);">of the individual's actions, both on others and on broader objectives. Discussing the impact helps the individual understand the consequences of their actions from a perspective other than their own. It's not about making them feel guilty but about fostering awareness and empathy.</span></p><p><strong style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);">Example</strong></p><p><em style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);">"These delays have caused some scheduling issues for the team, affecting our overall project timelines."</em></p>

## 👣 4. Next Steps

<p><span style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);">Finally, the </span><strong style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);">next steps</strong><span style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);"> are about proposing actions to address the issue. This should be a collaborative effort, focusing on solutions that are practical and achievable.</span></p><p><strong style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);">Example</strong></p><p><em style="color: rgb(55, 65, 81);">"Let's work together on steps to improve the situation."</em></p>

## 👉 Mistakes to avoid

<p>When using the COIN framework for feedback, it's important to avoid certain pitfalls to ensure the feedback is effective and constructive.</p><p>Here are some key mistakes to avoid:</p><ol><li><strong>Lacking Specificity.</strong> Avoid vague feedback. Be clear and specific in your observations and examples.</li><li><strong>Ignoring the Context.</strong> Consider the external factors or challenges that might have influenced the situation.</li><li><strong>Delayed Feedback.</strong> Address issues promptly to ensure relevance and clarity.</li><li><strong>Overloading with Information.</strong> Focus on a few critical issues rather than trying to cover everything at once.</li><li><strong>Not Involving the Recipient.</strong> Feedback should be a two-way conversation. Involve the recipient in discussing the issue and finding solutions.</li><li><strong>Failing to Follow Up.</strong> Regularly check in on progress and provide ongoing support to ensure feedback leads to improvement.</li></ol>

## 📖 Example

<p><strong>Context:</strong></p><p>"Sophia, I wanted to have a conversation about some recent challenges we've been facing in our project timelines. As you know, our agency has built a reputation for delivering creative and timely work, which is something we're all proud of."</p><p><strong>Observation:</strong></p><p>"Over the past three months, I've noticed that meeting project deadlines has been a struggle for you. Most notably, last Wednesday, the deadline for the major campaign launch was missed. This was the third such instance in recent weeks. Your creative input is always exceptional, but these delays are unusual for you."</p><p><strong>Impact:</strong></p><p>"This has had a significant impact. The client expressed dissatisfaction with our delivery time, which puts our relationship with them at risk. It also meant the rest of the team had to work extra hours to compensate, leading to increased costs for the project and a lot of fatigue and frustration within the team."</p><p><strong>Next Steps:</strong></p><p>"I want to understand what's been causing these challenges for you and how we can work together to overcome them. Is there support you need from the team or any obstacles you're facing that we can address?"</p>
