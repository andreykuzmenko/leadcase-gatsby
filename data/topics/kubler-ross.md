## 🥶 Response to changes

<p>Most people face change with fear. After all, their unpredictability threatens the loss of stability, control, status, competence, money, social connections, or jobs.</p><p>People's reactions to change manifest themselves in different emotions and defense mechanisms. Does this sound familiar? Then, you'll want to familiarize yourself with <strong>the Kübler-Ross model</strong>.</p>

## 😪 Kubler-Ross model

<!-- image: /images/cards/4979eeb43b6b649e.png -->

<p>Psychologist<strong> Elizabeth Kubler-Ross</strong>, who studied the emotional reactions of severely ill and dying people, identified <strong>five key stages</strong> they go through:</p><ol><li>Denial</li><li>Anger</li><li>Bargaining</li><li>Depression</li><li>Acceptance</li></ol><p>To some extent, people go through the same stages in their emotional reactions when they have to adapt to a new reality in the context of organizational changes.</p><p>Let's look at people's behavior and possible actions to manage the changes at each stage.</p>

## 🤭 1. Denial

<p><em>"I can't believe it," "It doesn't happen," "Not to me!", "Not again!"</em></p><p>In the initial denial phase, people fear that change will negatively affect them. If to pretend there is no change, if to distance ourselves from it, perhaps it will disappear.</p><p>Denial can manifest in people not participating in discussions and being indifferent or defiantly busy with routine-bureaucratic duties.</p><h3>What can be done at this point?</h3><ul><li>provide as much information as possible through different communication channels about the purpose and reasons for change;</li><li>give people time to understand the change;</li><li>stimulate discussions and get people involved.</li></ul>

## 😡 2. Anger

<p><em style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0);">"This is unfair! No! I can't accept this!"</em></p><p>At this stage, it is essential to understand that anger is provoked not by the changes themselves but by <strong>the losses</strong> they bring. As a result, people can:</p><ul><li>complain instead of working;</li><li>fall into accusations and criticism;</li><li>get irritated and cling to trifles.</li></ul><h3>What can be done at this stage?</h3><ul><li>listen to people, and recognize their feelings;</li><li>offer ways to compensate for the losses that the employees are afraid of, for example, additional training, retraining, flexible schedule, etc.;</li><li>encourage people to direct their working energy to the implementation of changes instead of criticism and empty words;</li><li>stop overt sabotage, but don't respond to aggression with aggression.</li></ul>

## 🙏 3. Bargaining

<p><em>"I'll do it on time, just a few more years."</em></p><p>Bargaining is an attempt to delay the inevitable. We attempt to “make a deal” with management or ourselves to put off change or to find a way out of a given situation. For example, a worker might begin to work overtime to avoid an impending layoff. On the other hand, <strong>bargaining</strong> is a sign that people are already starting to look toward the future and seek new opportunities.</p><h3>What can be done at this stage?</h3><ul><li>Direct people's energy into positive channels, not discouraging their ideas;</li><li>Stimulate brainstorms and strategic sessions;</li><li>Help employees appraise their careers and emerging opportunities in a different light.</li></ul>

## 🤒 4. Depression

<p><em>«What's the point in trying? It won't lead to anything good anyway.»</em></p><p>When people understand that bargaining does not result in changes that are beneficial to them, impending change becomes a reality. This pushes people into a state of discouragement, depression, and lack of energy. In companies, signs of <strong>depression</strong> are:</p><ul><li>general apathy;</li><li>an increase in sick leaves and absences at work;</li><li>high staff turnover.</li></ul><h3>What can be done at this stage?</h3><ul><li>acknowledge existing difficulties and problems;</li><li>eliminate any remaining fears, doubts, and indecision;</li><li>help people get out of the state of depression, support any attempts at active actions and provide positive feedback;</li><li>show employees personal examples of involvement in the change project;</li></ul>

## 😒 5. Adoption

<p><em>"It'll be okay," "I can't beat this, but I can prepare well for it."</em></p><p>While <strong>acceptance</strong> is the final stage, it is essential to understand that it does not mean acceptance. People realize that further resistance is pointless and begin to evaluate prospects.</p><p>Acceptance manifests itself as follows:</p><ul><li>ready to learn new things;</li><li>investing in making change work;</li><li>feeling engaged and engaging others.</li></ul><h3>What can you do at this point?</h3><ul><li>reinforce and reinforce new behaviors;</li><li>reward successes and achievements;</li><li>develop and set new goals.</li></ul>

## 👉 Understanding changes

<p>The Kubler-Ross model is useful for identifying and understanding how people cope with changes. In reality, <strong>people do not always go through the stages sequentially</strong>; there are often backslides to previous states. Furthermore, not everybody reaches the acceptance stage.</p><p>Change Leaders in organizations, who know this emotional dynamic, have several advantages:</p><ul><li>understand that <strong>resistance is normal</strong>;</li><li>are aware of at what stage the resistance lies and what reactions can be expected further;</li><li>feel relief from understanding that their reactions and feelings are normal and not a sign of weakness;</li><li>can devise and execute the appropriate actions for a fast and effective progression through these stages.</li></ul>
