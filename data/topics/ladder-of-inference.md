## ❌ Errors in decision making

<p>In our fast-paced age, people don't have time to think carefully about information, so most of us jump to conclusions. At best, we're corrected; at worst, it can lead to a loss of money or a deterioration in our relationship with others. It also works the other way around-your interlocutor may draw the wrong conclusions, and if you don't recognize that, you may have the incorrect information.</p><p><strong>The Ladder of Conclusions</strong> helps you draw the correct conclusions and have constructive discussions with people.</p>

## 💡 The Ladder of Inference

<!-- image: /images/cards/25ad8d7cd2aafd13.png -->

<p><span style="color: rgba(0, 0, 0, 0.87);">Chris Argyris</span>, the author of the ladder of inference, identifies <strong>seven fundamental steps</strong> we go through from information to action:</p><ol><li>Obtaining data</li><li>Selecting data</li><li>Interpreting data</li><li>Building assumptions</li><li>Making conclusions</li><li>Assuming beliefs</li><li>Acting</li></ol>

## 📈 1. Obtaining data

<p>You are surrounded by reality and facts, and the first step on the ladder of inference is to observe this information.</p><p><strong>Example</strong></p><p><em>A colleague is 20 minutes late, looking exhausted and frustrated. Without giving a reason, he joins the meeting.</em></p>

## 📋 2. Selecting data

<p>We very rarely use all the available facts to make decisions. We tend to focus on specific parts of information or even certain parts that better fit our preferences or agenda.</p><p><strong>Example</strong></p><p><em>We focus </em><strong><em>only </em></strong><em>on</em><strong><em> </em></strong><em>the fact that a colleague is 20 minutes late</em></p>

## 🧐 3. Interpreting data

<p>At this stage, we give meaning to this information based on our past experiences, biases, and beliefs.</p><p><strong>Example</strong></p><p><em>In the past, you have had to deal with a colleague who did not respect schedules and deadlines and was challenging to work with.</em></p>

## 🤔 4. Building assumptions

<p>When we attach importance to selected data, it enables us to make assumptions, ignore all other facts, and jump to conclusions about what's happening.</p><p><strong>Example</strong></p><p><em>Your colleague is late because he doesn't take the schedule seriously.</em></p>

## 📝 5. Making conclusions

<p>As a result, we transform our assumptions into firm conclusions - again, without considering all the facts we might have been presented with in the beginning.</p><p><strong>Example</strong></p><p><em>Your colleague needs to be reprimanded and reminded that he must come to work on time. Otherwise, it will become an even bigger problem.</em></p>

## 🙌 6. Accepting beliefs

<p>This is where problems can arise: <strong>We adopt beliefs about the situation based on the conclusions we have drawn</strong>. We then use those beliefs and experiences to form future judgments about similar scenarios.</p><p><strong>Example</strong></p><p><em>Any employee who is late pays no attention to schedules and credentials and should be treated accordingly.</em></p>

## 🚴 7. Acting

<p>Finally, based on our beliefs, we take actions that feel right to us. Then our actions change the situation and create a new set of circumstances.</p><p><strong>Example</strong></p><p><em>As a result, you tell your colleague's supervisor about the situation, asking him to take action.</em></p>

## 🔄 Reflexive cycle

<p>Between step two (Selecting data) and step six (Accepting beliefs), there is something called the <strong>reflexive cycle</strong>, which means that the beliefs we form will influence what data we select the next time we find ourselves in a similar scenario.</p><p><strong>Example</strong></p><p><em>Since you had a negative interaction with a previous colleague who didn't show up on time, you now tend to use only selected data, focusing on lateness.</em></p><p>Unfortunately, this can create a <strong>toxic cycle</strong> that causes us to constantly act on limited information and misconceptions about what is happening.</p><p><em>In the example above, your colleague may have been late because of a sudden visit to the doctor with the baby.</em></p>

## 🎢 Descending stairs

<p>The ladder of reasoning assumes that you can influence and take responsibility for your decisions. In other words, you can stop before you even take action.</p><h3>Steps for going down the ladder:</h3><ol><li><strong>Actions</strong>: Why do these actions or decisions seem right? What are the alternatives?</li><li><strong>Beliefs</strong>: What are my beliefs about this? What are they based on? What do I believe?</li><li><strong>Conclusions</strong>: Why have I reached these conclusions? What assumptions are they based on?</li><li><strong>Expectations</strong>: Are my assumptions valid? Why do I think so?</li><li><strong>Interpretation</strong>: Am I looking at the data objectively? What else might they mean?</li><li><strong>Sample data</strong>: What other potentially essential data am I missing? Are there facts that I am intentionally ignoring? What other sources of information can help me?</li></ol>

## 🤯 Slow down

<p>Often we draw scenarios in our heads that have little to do with reality. They are imaginary, but they can hurt us and those around us.</p><p>Try to avoid such situations by simply slowing down a little. Yes, our lives are dynamic and unpredictable, but that doesn't mean we can't be objective. After all, no one demands that we make decisions in a split second.</p><p>As a rule, we always have at least a few minutes. And in that time, you can certainly set aside your assumptions and preconceptions to make the right decision.</p>
